Track J — what Josh does once

You don't need to do anything in this chapter — it's here so you know what's happening behind the scenes before you start Track P. Josh does these six things, once, in this order, before handing the rest over to you.

  1. Publishes the engine — the software itself becomes public and open-source.

    What this means The code that runs your website (not your content — just the underlying software) becomes freely available under an open licence called MIT. This is what makes it possible for you to keep your own independent copy that nobody can take away.

    You know it worked when… Josh tells you the engine's GitHub page is public and readable by anyone.

  2. Provisions a small, temporary "drill kit" — a practice run, on Josh's own money.

    What this means Before doing anything with your real accounts, Josh rehearses this entire guide end-to-end on a set of throwaway test accounts (a test website address, a temporary server, a small pre-approved AI budget — about £10–15 total, on Josh's own card, agreed with him in advance). This finds and fixes any confusing steps before you ever see them.

    You know it worked when… Josh tells you the practice run is done and the guide has been corrected against anything that tripped him up.

  3. Runs a backup of your website's current state, from Josh's side.

    What this means A safety copy of everything about your site — the pages, the images, the settings — is taken and stored in a private backup location, before anything else changes. Your backup custody starts before your new hosting does.

    You know it worked when… Josh confirms the first backup snapshot exists.

  4. Starts the transfer of your website's code repository to your organisation, and creates a new private one for your own business documents.

    What this means GitHub is where all the code and content for your site lives. Josh starts a transfer of the existing one into your organisation (you'll accept it in Track P, chapter 2) and creates a brand new, private repository called ca-business for documents that were only ever useful to the running of your business, not to the website's code — things like your own photos, your original brief, and your Google reviews. Josh moves that material out of the public engine code and into this new private repository, using the exact commands below, so nothing personal ends up somewhere public by mistake.
    git checkout 7c4fa3c02957599bfed994ddb37a93ed293e685f -- \ photos/ advertising/ brief.md \ docs/DESIGN-AND-CONTENT.md docs/google-reviews.json \ docs/booking-platform-comparison.md reviews-demo.html \ tooling/downscale_photos.py

    You know it worked when… Josh shows you the new ca-business repository containing your photos and documents, and confirms the public engine code no longer contains them.

  5. Lowers the time-to-live (TTL) on your domain's DNS records at your registrar.

    What this means This is a small technical setting that controls how quickly changes to your website's address take effect elsewhere on the internet. Lowering it now (a few days before "Go live") means that when the final switch happens in Track P, chapter 8, it takes effect quickly everywhere, instead of slowly over the following day or two.

    You know it worked when… Josh confirms the TTL has been lowered and it's safe to schedule "Go live."

  6. Does the final DNS flip with you, live, on the phone.

    What this means This is the one moment that changes what your visitors see — covered fully in Track P, chapter 8 ("Go live"), which you'll do together.

    You know it worked when… You've read Track P, chapter 8 — you'll do this step together when you both get there, not before.
