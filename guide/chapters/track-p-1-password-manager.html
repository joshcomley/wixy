Track P, chapter 1 — Set up a password manager

Over the next few chapters you're going to create quite a few new accounts — GitHub, DigitalOcean, Cloudflare, Anthropic — each with its own password, and sometimes a second credential too. This short chapter gets you a proper, secure place to keep them all.

If you already use a password manager you're happy with, skip straight ahead to Track P, chapter 2 — there's no need for a second one. If not, it's worth the ten minutes: every credential this guide has you create comes with an instruction to save it under an exact name, so you can always find it again later. Josh never sees or holds a copy of any of them — by design, they're entirely yours: not even he could recover them for you if you lost them.

Two well-known, reputable options, if a recommendation helps: Bitwarden has a genuinely free personal plan (unlimited passwords and devices, no time limit), and 1Password is a popular paid option with a similarly polished feel. Either is a good choice — or use one you already know and trust. This guide doesn't mind which.

  1. Create your password manager account.

    Where On whichever provider's website you've chosen, click "Create free account", "Get started", or "Sign up", and follow the prompts: your email address, and a master password.
    Your master password is the one exception in this whole guide — it's the one credential you can't save in a password manager, because nothing else is protecting it. Choose something strong but memorable (three or four random words work well), and if you'd like a backup, write it on paper and keep it somewhere safe, like a drawer — never on your computer.

    You know it worked when… you're signed in and looking at your new, empty vault.

  2. Install the browser extension.

    Where Search your browser's own extension store (Chrome Web Store, Firefox Add-ons, or Microsoft Edge Add-ons — whichever browser you use) for your password manager's name, and click "Add" or "Get". Once it's installed, click its new icon near your browser's address bar and sign in with the account you just created.

    You know it worked when… the extension's icon in your browser toolbar shows you as signed in, rather than prompting you to log in.

  3. Save a test entry, to prove the whole thing works.

    Where In the extension (or on your vault's website), click the "+" or "Add item" button, give it any name — "Test entry" is fine — and save it.

    You know it worked when… the test entry appears in your vault's list. Delete it afterwards if you like — it's only there to prove the loop works before you rely on it for anything real.
