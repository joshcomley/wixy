Track P, chapter 4 — Your Cloudflare account and tunnel

Cloudflare does two jobs for you: it manages your domain's address book — which computer people reach when they type your web address — and it opens a secure, one-way tunnel from your droplet to the internet, so your website is reachable without ever leaving an open door on your server. This is the most involved chapter after GitHub; every step here is safe to pause on if you need a break partway through.

  1. Create your Cloudflare account.

    Where Go to cloudflare.com and choose "Sign up." An email address and password is enough — this account itself is free, no card needed yet.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — Cloudflare account".

    You know it worked when… you're looking at the Cloudflare dashboard, with no websites added yet.

  2. Add your domain to Cloudflare.

    Where From the dashboard, choose "Add a domain" (sometimes shown as "+ Add site") and type your domain exactly as you own it. Cloudflare scans your existing settings and then shows you a choice of plans — pick Free, which is everything this guide needs.

    You know it worked when… your domain appears in Cloudflare's list of websites, and it shows you two nameservers to copy, both ending in .ns.cloudflare.com.

  3. Point your domain at Cloudflare by updating its nameservers at the company you registered it with.

    Where This company is called your registrar. Log in there, find its DNS or nameserver settings, and replace whatever is currently listed with the two Cloudflare nameservers you copied in the previous step.
    Your site keeps working normally throughout this. This step only moves who manages your domain's settings — it doesn't change what your visitors see today. That only happens right at the very end of this guide ("Go live"), which you'll do together with Josh, and even that can be undone in minutes.

    You know it worked when… your registrar shows the two Cloudflare nameservers saved (Cloudflare itself may take a while to notice and mark your domain "active" there — that's fine, and nothing you need to wait for before continuing).

  4. Turn on Cloudflare Zero Trust and choose a team name.

    Where In the Cloudflare dashboard, find "Zero Trust" in the navigation — it opens its own section of the site. The first time you go in, it asks you to choose a team name; this becomes part of an address you'll use shortly, like cottage-aesthetics.cloudflareaccess.com, so pick something recognisable, lower-case, with no spaces. You may also be asked for a payment method at this point £0 — that's normal Cloudflare behaviour for this section, not a hidden charge; the plan you want is Free.

    You know it worked when… you're looking at the Zero Trust dashboard — a different-looking area from the main Cloudflare site.

  5. Create a Tunnel and copy the token it gives you.

    Where In the Zero Trust dashboard, look for "Networks," then "Tunnels" (Cloudflare moves this occasionally — if you don't see "Networks," check under "Access," or use the search box at the top of the page). Choose "Create a tunnel," pick the "Cloudflared" connector, and name it something recognisable like "cottage-aesthetics." On the next page, Cloudflare shows you an install command containing a long string after the word --token — that string, on its own, is what you need.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — Cloudflare Tunnel token".

    You know it worked when… your new tunnel appears in the Tunnels list, and you've copied its token somewhere safe.

  6. Note down your Zero Trust team domain.

    Where Still in the Zero Trust dashboard, open "Settings," then "General" — there's a field called "Team domain," showing the full address based on the team name you chose earlier, ending in .cloudflareaccess.com. Copy the whole thing.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — Cloudflare team domain".

    You know it worked when… you have the full address saved, ending in .cloudflareaccess.com.

  7. Create the Access application that protects your admin area.

    Where In the Zero Trust dashboard, find "Access," then "Applications," and choose "Add an application," then "Self-hosted." Give it a name like "Cottage Aesthetics admin." For the application domain, enter your domain with the path /admin; there's an option nearby to add a second domain — use it to also add your domain with the path /api/admin (both need protecting: the second is what your admin screen's own behind-the-scenes traffic uses, and the admin screen won't work correctly without it). Further into the same wizard, add one policy — call it something like "Owner and Josh," set it to Allow, and under its rule choose "Emails," then list exactly two addresses: your own, and Josh's. Save through to the end of the wizard.
    Josh's email stays on this list for now so he can still help you if you ever need it — this is entirely your choice to keep or remove, and Appendix C shows you exactly how to take it off whenever you're ready.

    You know it worked when… your new application appears in the Access "Applications" list, showing both paths and a policy with two email addresses.

  8. Copy the application's Audience tag.

    Where Open the application you just created from the "Applications" list — its overview shows a field called "Application Audience (AUD) Tag," a long string of letters and numbers, with its own copy button.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — Cloudflare Access AUD tag".

    You know it worked when… you have the full tag saved, copied straight from that field rather than retyped by hand.

  9. Do a final check that everything from this chapter is safely saved.

    Check Open your password manager and look for three new entries from today: your Cloudflare Tunnel token, your Cloudflare team domain, and your Cloudflare Access AUD tag. Chapter 6 asks for these three back, by name, so having them ready now saves you a trip back here later.

    You know it worked when… all three are saved, and your domain registrar shows Cloudflare's two nameservers in place (even if Cloudflare hasn't marked your domain "active" yet — that isn't blocking you).
