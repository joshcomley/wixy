Track P, chapter 2 — Your GitHub account and organisation

GitHub is where the code behind your website lives — the filing cabinet and history book for the software that runs it. This chapter gets you your own account there, your own organisation to hold everything in — your site's code, the engine underneath it, your business documents — and a handful of one-off settings switched on so the later chapters go smoothly.

  1. Create your own personal GitHub account.

    Where Go to github.com/join. Enter your email address, choose a password and a username, and confirm your email address when GitHub asks you to. This is your individual, personal login — the shared home for your website's code comes next, in step 2.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — GitHub (personal account)".

    You know it worked when… you're signed in and see your own GitHub homepage, with your username shown in the top right.

  2. Create a GitHub organisation to hold everything.

    Where Click the "+" menu near your profile photo (top right of any GitHub page) and choose "New organization". Pick the Free plan, then, on the form that follows, choose "My personal account" when asked who it belongs to, and use the text below for the organisation's name — if that exact name's already taken, a close variant such as cottage-aesthetics-uk is fine.
    cottage-aesthetics
    This is why an organisation, rather than just using your personal account directly: everything else in this guide — your site's code, the engine software, your business documents — lives inside it, not under your own personal login. That's what lets you cleanly add Josh to it now as a helper, and just as cleanly remove him later if you ever want to, without touching your own account at all (Appendix C).

    You know it worked when… you land on your new organisation's page, with its name at the top and an empty list of repositories underneath.

  3. Accept the transfer of your website's existing code into your new organisation.

    Where Josh already started this, in Track J. GitHub emails you when a transfer is waiting — open the email and follow its link. If you can't find the email, click the bell icon (top right of any GitHub page) for your GitHub notifications instead; either route leads to an invitation page naming the repository and a big "Accept" button.

    You know it worked when… a repository named cottage-aesthetics-site appears in your organisation's repository list.

  4. Confirm your new private business-documents repository is there too.

    Where On your organisation's page, look through the repository list for one named ca-business, with a small padlock or "Private" label next to its name — Josh created this one alongside the transfer, for your photos, brief, and other business documents that have nothing to do with the website's code.

    You know it worked when… you can open ca-business and see folders and files inside it, such as photos/ or brief.md.

  5. Make your own copy of the engine software — a "fork" — inside your organisation.

    Where Go to github.com/joshcomley/wixy and click "Fork" (top right, next to Star and Watch). On the "Create a new fork" page, change the Owner dropdown from your personal account to your new organisation, and change the Repository name to the text below — later chapters, and the setup script you'll run in chapter 6, refer to "your engine fork" and expect this exact name. Leave everything else as it is and click "Create fork".
    wixy-engine

    You know it worked when… you land on a new repository whose owner, shown top-left, is your organisation — not joshcomley.

  6. Switch on Actions for your new fork.

    Where On your wixy-engine repository, click the "Actions" tab. GitHub switches this off automatically on every new fork, as a spam-prevention measure, so you'll see a banner reading something like "Workflows aren't being run on this forked repository" with a button labelled "I understand my workflows, go ahead and enable them" — click it.

    You know it worked when… the banner disappears and the Actions tab shows a (currently empty) list of workflow runs instead.

  7. Switch on the weekly update-check workflow specifically.

    Where Still on the Actions tab, find "Sync from upstream" in the list of workflows on the left. Even with Actions enabled generally, GitHub can leave a scheduled workflow like this one switched off separately — if its page shows a message that it's disabled, click the "Enable workflow" button.
    This is the check that runs every Monday morning and tells you when Josh's engine has updates available. It's the only way you'd find out — without it switched on, updates could be sitting there and you'd simply never be told.

    You know it worked when… "Sync from upstream"'s own page no longer shows a disabled message.

  8. Allow fine-grained access tokens in your organisation's settings.

    Where On your organisation's page, go to Settings, then find "Third-party Access" in the left sidebar → "Personal access tokens" → "Settings". Make sure fine-grained personal access tokens are allowed, and switch off any "require administrator approval" option you see.
    A later chapter has you create one of these tokens for your AI assistant. If administrator approval is left switched on, that token would sit waiting for an approval click you'd only end up giving yourself anyway — switching it off now saves you that extra round trip.

    You know it worked when… the settings page shows fine-grained personal access tokens as allowed, with administrator approval not required.

  9. Turn on branch protection for your site repository's main branch.

    Where Open your cottage-aesthetics-site repository, click "Settings" along its top menu, then "Branches" in the left sidebar. Click "Add branch protection rule" (it may just say "Add rule", depending on when you're reading this), and type main into the branch name pattern field. Tick "Require a pull request before merging" and "Require status checks to pass before merging" — if the box for choosing a specific check looks empty, that's fine, it fills in once a check has actually run; the tick itself is what matters. Do not add yourself, or anyone, to any list of people allowed to bypass these rules. Click "Create" (or "Save changes") to finish.
    This one's required, not optional — chapter 6 pauses and waits for you to confirm it's done, for both this repository and the next step's, before it carries on. The reason: it's what stops even a leaked AI-assistant credential from ever being able to push straight to your live site's source — turning "the AI assistant can only open a pull request" from a convention into something GitHub itself enforces, no matter what.

    You know it worked when… a rule for main appears in the repository's branch protection list.

  10. Repeat the same branch protection on your engine fork.

    Where Open your wixy-engine repository and repeat the previous step exactly: Settings → Branches → add a rule for main, tick the same two boxes, and add no bypass actors.

    You know it worked when… both cottage-aesthetics-site and wixy-engine show a branch protection rule on main — that's this chapter done.
