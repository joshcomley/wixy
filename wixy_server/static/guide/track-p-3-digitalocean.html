Track P — Purdi · 15 minutes

Track P, chapter 3 — Your DigitalOcean account and droplet

This is where your website will actually live from now on: a small computer of your own, rented by the month from a hosting company called DigitalOcean. This chapter gets that computer switched on and ready — the next chapter connects it to the internet, and chapter 6 puts your website onto it.

You won't need to install anything. Everything in this guide — including the single command you'll paste in chapter 6 — happens inside DigitalOcean's own website, in a black, terminal-style window that runs right there in your browser tab. There's no separate SSH software to find, install, or learn.

  1. Create your DigitalOcean account.

    Where Go to digitalocean.com and choose "Sign up." An email address and password (or signing up with your Google account) is enough to get started — but before you can create anything, you'll also be asked to add a card. DigitalOcean is not a free service: go in expecting a recurring charge of about $12 US a month (roughly £9.50, since DigitalOcean bills in dollars) once you create your droplet in the next step.
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — DigitalOcean account".

    You know it worked when… you're looking at the DigitalOcean dashboard (sometimes called the "Control Panel"), ready to create your first server.

  2. Start creating a droplet, choosing the exact image, plan, and region below.

    Where Click "Create," then "Droplets." Under "Choose an image," switch to the Marketplace tab, search for "Docker," and pick the one called "Docker on Ubuntu." (Not plain "Ubuntu" on its own — the Marketplace version comes with software your droplet needs already installed.) Under "Choose a region," pick London. Under "Choose Size," stay on the Basic tab, then Regular, and pick the row showing 2 GB memory / 1 vCPU ~£9.50/mo. Leave Backups, Monitoring, and any extra volumes switched off underneath — you don't need any of them; Wixy has its own backup system, covered later in this guide.

    You know it worked when… you can see the 2 GB / 1 vCPU row selected, with "Docker on Ubuntu" and "London" chosen above it.

  3. Set a password for the droplet, then finish creating it.

    Where Further down the same page, under "Authentication," choose the Password tab (not "SSH keys" — you won't need one). Type a strong password, or use DigitalOcean's own "Generate password" option if it's offered. You can leave the hostname as it is, or rename it to something recognisable like "cottage-aesthetics." Scroll to the bottom and click "Create Droplet."
    Save this in your password manager as "Cottage Aesthetics — DigitalOcean droplet password".

    You know it worked when… DigitalOcean takes you to the droplet's own page, showing it "starting up."

  4. Wait for the droplet to finish setting itself up.

    Where Stay on the droplet's page, or find it again from your list of Droplets — this usually takes well under a minute. You don't need to note down its address (its "IP address"): you'll only ever reach this droplet through DigitalOcean's own browser console, and because it only ever reaches out to the internet — through a secure tunnel you'll set up in the next chapter — rather than waiting for anything to reach in, it doesn't need a fixed address at all. If DigitalOcean offers you a "Reserved IP" anywhere, you can skip it.

    You know it worked when… the droplet's status shows "Active."
