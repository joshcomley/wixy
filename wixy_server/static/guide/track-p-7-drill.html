Track P — Purdi

Track P, chapter 7 — The drill

Everything is installed. This chapter proves it all actually works — publishing, updates, and your AI assistant — on a temporary, private test address that only you (and Josh, if you've kept him on) can even reach. Nothing about your real, live website changes in this chapter; that's chapter 8, once you're confident.

  1. Add a temporary test address, pointed at your droplet through your tunnel.

    Where In the Cloudflare Zero Trust dashboard, go to "Networks," then "Tunnels" (the same place you created your tunnel in chapter 4), and click on its name. Find the "Public Hostname" tab, then "Add a public hostname." For "Subdomain," type test; for "Domain," choose your domain from the dropdown; leave the path blank. Under "Service," choose type HTTP, and for the URL, type the address below exactly.
    wixy:9380

    You know it worked when… your new hostname appears in the Public Hostname list, showing test.yourdomain routed to wixy:9380.

  2. Protect this test address's admin area too, the same way your real one already is.

    Where In Zero Trust, go to "Access," then "Applications," and open the application you created in chapter 4 ("Cottage Aesthetics admin" or similar). Click "Edit," and in its list of application domains, add two more: your new test address with /admin, and again with /api/admin — exactly like the two you added in chapter 4, just with test. in front this time. Save.
    Skipping this step would leave your test admin area reachable by anyone who guessed the address, with no login at all — this is what closes that gap, using the exact same two people (you and Josh) already trusted on your real one.

    You know it worked when… the application's domain list shows four entries in total: your real domain's /admin and /api/admin, and now your test address's matching pair.

  3. Visit your test address and confirm your website loads.

    Where In any browser, go to https://test. followed by your domain (for example https://test.yourdomain.co.uk). Give it a minute if it doesn't load first try — the tunnel and Cloudflare both need a few moments to notice the new address after step 1.

    You know it worked when… your website appears, looking exactly like the live one — because it's the same site, just not the address the public knows about yet.

  4. Visit the test address's admin area and sign in.

    Where Add /admin to the same address (e.g. https://test.yourdomain.co.uk/admin). Cloudflare Access stops you here and asks for your email; enter it, then enter the one-time code it emails you.

    You know it worked when… you land on your familiar admin screen — draft editing, chat panel, Settings, all present.

  5. Make a small test change and publish it.

    Where Edit any piece of text on any page — something harmless and easy to spot, like adding the word "test" somewhere unused — then use the Publish button as you normally would.

    You know it worked when… refreshing https://test.yourdomain.co.uk (no /admin) shows your change, live. Feel free to undo it with History afterwards.

  6. Check for engine updates.

    Where In the admin area, go to Settings, then the "Engine" card. It shows your current version and whether any updates are available. If it offers one, click "Get engine updates" and watch it progress through checking, building, and deploying — usually a couple of minutes. If it already says "You're up to date," that's a pass too; there's nothing else to do here.
    If an update ever causes a problem, the same card has an "Undo last update" button that puts things back exactly as they were, in one click — you don't need to use it now, just know it's there.

    You know it worked when… the Engine card shows "You're up to date," whether or not it needed to do anything to get there.

  7. Ask your AI assistant to make a small change, and watch it become a real, published edit.

    Where Open the chat panel and ask for something simple — "change the word 'Welcome' to 'Hello' at the top of the home page" is a good test. The assistant works in the background and, when it's done, leaves you a link to review its suggested change on GitHub as a pull request. Open that link, check the change looks right, and click "Merge."

    You know it worked when… after merging, the change appears in your admin area as a new draft — publish it the normal way, and it's live on your test address too.

  8. Note where your backup status lives, for later.

    Where In Settings, the "System" card shows the age of your last automatic backup, along with your disk usage and current engine version. Your very first backup runs tonight, so there's nothing to see there yet — this step is just so you know where to look afterwards, and to recognise the warning banner (backup older than 48 hours) if you ever see it.

    You know it worked when… you can find the System card and understand what it's telling you — nothing to check off here until tomorrow.
